The Burn Cluster: When the Data Finds the Children First

At Children's National Medical Center, Jefferson applied GIS analysis to pediatric injury data and found what no one else had seen. This is the story of why he does what he does.

Organization
Children's National Medical Center
Role
Clinical Informatics Analyst → Manager, BI & Clinical Analytics
Methods
GIS, Geospatial Analysis, Population Health Analytics
Location
Washington, D.C. metro area

The Discovery

Jefferson was working with pediatric injury data at Children's National when he noticed something that didn't fit the expected distribution. The burn cases weren't random. They were clustering — multiple children, treated at the same hospital, living in the same buildings.

He applied geographic information system (GIS) analysis to map the home addresses against the injury data. The cluster became visible immediately: a handful of apartment buildings in the same neighborhood, owned by the same landlord, producing a disproportionate number of pediatric burn injuries.

The cause, when investigated: water heaters set at dangerously high temperatures. Not one accident. A pattern. And a pattern with a single, identifiable, correctable cause.

The data found the children before the system did.
No individual clinician seeing one child at a time would have identified the pattern. The insight only existed at the aggregate level — in the data, if you knew how to look.

Why This Story Matters

This discovery is the earliest, clearest statement of Jefferson's professional thesis: data, applied rigorously and with the right questions, can find what individual human observation misses. And when it finds something, the obligation is to act.

The GIS analysis at Children's National wasn't a research project. It was a practice — the habit of asking "what does the geographic distribution of this data tell us that a chart doesn't?" That habit, developed in a pediatric hospital analyzing injury data, became the foundation of everything Jefferson built afterward.

The AllVax equity monitoring. The COVID death disparity analysis at Lake County. The cost-effectiveness model for disease surveillance in his doctoral research. All of it traces back to a moment when Jefferson looked at a map of burn injuries and asked why they were all coming from the same buildings.

The Method, Applied More Broadly

After the burn cluster discovery, Jefferson formalized the approach: using geographic information that had never before been applied to disease treatment and prevention. He created novel population health strategies that mapped clinical data against social determinants — neighborhood, housing type, proximity to environmental hazards, access to care.

This was early-stage work in what is now recognized as geospatial public health analytics. Jefferson was doing it at a pediatric hospital in Washington, D.C. before the field had a common name.

What This Shows

The burn cluster story is the answer to the question every hiring manager is really asking: Why does this person do what they do?

Jefferson does what he does because he has seen — literally, in the data — what happens when systems fail to see patterns that are right there if you look correctly. He builds the tools that let organizations look correctly. He trains the instinct to ask the geographic question, the population question, the "why is this distributed this way" question.

That's not a credential. It's a philosophy, formed at the intersection of a data set and a child who got hurt.
