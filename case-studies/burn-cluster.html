The Burn Cluster: When the Data Finds the Children First

At Children's National, I applied GIS to clinical data in a way nobody at the institution had done before. The result wasn't a dashboard. It was a discovery that traced a pattern of pediatric burn injuries back to one landlord — and directly informed protective action for the children in those buildings.

Organization
Children's National Medical Center
Role
Clinical Informatics Analyst → Manager, BI & Clinical Analytics
Methods
GIS, Geospatial Analysis, Population Health Analytics, Clinical Decision Support
Timeline
2012 – April 2016

The Problem

Pediatric burn injuries were appearing in the emergency department. The clinical record captured what had happened to each child — the type of burn, the severity, the treatment. But the clinical record alone couldn't explain why these cases were appearing in clusters, or what was causing them.

No one at Children's National was applying geospatial methods to clinical outcomes. The infrastructure for doing so — population health registries, a data warehouse that could be queried across patients, the analytical framework for asking geographic questions — didn't exist yet. I was building it.

The Decision

I had been building out the institution's first real population health infrastructure. As that work matured, it became possible to ask questions that hadn't been answerable before: not just what is happening to individual patients, but what patterns exist across the patient population, and what do those patterns tell us about the communities these children come from?

The decision to apply GIS methods to the burn injury data came from a simple observation: the clinical data was telling an incomplete story. The children were coming from somewhere. The addresses were in the record. If I could map them, I could see what the clinical data alone couldn't show.

What I Built

The infrastructure that made this discovery possible wasn't the GIS analysis — it was everything that came before it. Population health registries that captured patient data in a consistent, queryable format. A data warehouse strategy that made it possible to run population-level queries across the patient record. Clinical decision support tools that surfaced the right information at the right moment.

The geospatial analysis was possible because the infrastructure existed to support it. That's the point: data infrastructure is not valuable because of any single query. It's valuable because of the questions it makes possible that nobody had thought to ask yet.

The Result

  • Identified a geographic cluster of pediatric burn injuries concentrated in specific apartment buildings in the D.C. metro area
  • Traced the pattern to one landlord, multiple buildings, water heaters set dangerously high — a systemic hazard invisible in the clinical record
  • Data directly informed protective action for children in those buildings
  • Patent received for work completed during this tenure at Children's National
  • Established clinical decision support infrastructure that preceded the industry-wide push for population health tools by several years
"The clinical data told me what happened to each child. The map told me why it kept happening. Those are two different questions, and only one of them lets you actually stop it."
— Jefferson McMillan-Wilhoit

Why This Story Matters

This is not the case study with the largest numbers. It doesn't have a 30× metric or a 40,000-person headline. What it has is the clearest example of what I mean when I say I find where a system fails a human being.

The system — the clinical data system, the building inspection system, the landlord accountability system — was failing those children. The data infrastructure I built made it possible to see that failure in a way that could be acted on. That is what this work is for.

Every role since has been a version of the same thing: build the infrastructure that makes the right questions answerable, then ask them.
