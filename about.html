The Story

Where it started.

I grew up interested in how things work — and in what happens when they don't. My undergraduate degree from Embry-Riddle Aeronautical University was in aeronautics, with a concentration in human factors: the study of how human beings interact with complex systems under pressure, and where those interactions break down.

My career path shifted before it reached the Air Force. But the lens I developed at Embry-Riddle — find where the system fails the human, then fix it — has driven every decision I've made since.

After Embry-Riddle I joined the NTSB doing transportation safety research. That led to health informatics at Children's National Medical Center in Washington, D.C., where I built clinical decision support tools and applied geospatial methods to pediatric population health in ways the institution hadn't done before. From Children's National I went to Northwestern, leading the data strategy for a complex EHR integration across four systems and 21 care locations. From there to Lake County Health Department as Director of Health Informatics, then promoted to CIO and CDO. I built four departments from scratch. Then COVID arrived, and in 8 weeks we built AllVax — a vaccine orchestration platform that registered 40,000 community members in its first 24 hours.

In 2022, I co-founded Flourish & Thrive Labs with my wife, Juliana. I'm the CEO and the architect of record for PH360 — a 7-layer, HIPAA-compliant, multi-tenant public health AI platform built on AWS. We grew revenue 30× in our first year. I'm currently completing my DrPH at Johns Hopkins Bloomberg School of Public Health.

A Defining Moment

The burn cluster.

At Children's National, I was building out the institution's first real population health infrastructure — registries, a data warehouse, clinical decision support. I started mapping patient data geographically, looking for patterns that the clinical record alone couldn't show.

What I found was a cluster of pediatric burn injuries concentrated in specific apartment buildings. Children coming into the ED with scalding burns, from families who didn't know each other, all from the same few blocks. The clinical data couldn't explain it. The map could.

It traced back to one landlord, multiple buildings, water heaters set dangerously high. The data directly informed protective action. Those children didn't get burned again.

That is still the clearest example I have of what data infrastructure can do when it's built to ask the right questions. It's why I do this work.
