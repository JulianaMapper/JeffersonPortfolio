PH360: Building the Platform the Market Was Missing

Jefferson had been the CIO who needed this platform and couldn't find it. So he built it — 7 layers, HIPAA-compliant, multi-tenant, on AWS. This is what it takes to architect something from scratch when you've been on both sides of the table.

The Problem

As CIO at Lake County Health Department, Jefferson spent four years experiencing firsthand what was broken in public health technology infrastructure. Local health departments were running on systems that couldn't talk to each other, couldn't scale, and weren't built for the people who actually needed to use them.

When he went looking for a platform that could modernize public health data infrastructure — something cloud-native, AI-enabled, HIPAA-compliant, and actually usable by under-resourced local health departments — he couldn't find it. The enterprise health IT vendors built for hospital systems. The public health vendors built legacy software that looked like it hadn't changed since 2003. Nobody had built the thing the market needed.

So Jefferson built it himself.

The Architecture Decision

The core technical decision was AWS — not because it was the default, but because it was the right choice for the specific problem. Public health departments needed a platform that was compliant-by-design (not bolted-on), that could be multi-tenant across different jurisdictions with strict data separation, and that could support AI capabilities without requiring a dedicated data science team to operate.

Jefferson designed PH360 as a 7-layer architecture:

  • Presentation layer — user-facing interfaces built for non-technical public health staff
  • API layer — AWS API Gateway managing all external integrations and authentication
  • Application logic layer — Lambda functions for serverless, scalable processing
  • AI/ML layer — AWS Bedrock with Claude LLM for RAG-based knowledge retrieval and automated workflows
  • Data processing layer — automated pipelines for public health data ingestion and normalization
  • Storage layer — S3 with appropriate encryption and access controls for HIPAA compliance
  • Identity & security layer — Cognito for multi-tenant authentication with strict data isolation

Every layer was designed to be compliant-by-default — HIPAA requirements weren't added after the fact, they were the architectural constraints the system was built around.

The difference between "oversight" and "architect of record"
Jefferson didn't lead a team that built PH360. He wrote the infrastructure. That distinction matters — it means he understands every failure mode, every scaling decision, and every compliance tradeoff at the level of someone who has debugged it at 2am.

The Business Reality

Building the platform was one challenge. Building the business while building the platform was another. Jefferson was simultaneously operating as CEO, CFO, CRO, and CIO of F&T Labs — and as the sole architect of PH360's infrastructure.

He led the SBA 8(a) and WBE certification processes, which positioned F&T Labs for government contracting and opened the public health agency market in a way that wasn't accessible to most early-stage health tech companies. He built the RFP response capability, the partner relationships, and the client delivery model — while shipping code.

Revenue grew from $22,000 to over $500,000 in the first 12 months. 30× year-over-year growth, with no outside investment.

What This Shows

PH360 is the fullest expression of Jefferson's thesis: the operator who became the builder, because he'd been the person who needed what didn't exist. Every architectural decision in PH360 was informed by years of sitting in the CIO seat and understanding what public health technology actually needed to do — not what looked good in a product demo.

That context doesn't come from a computer science degree. It comes from being accountable for a county's health outcomes when a system goes down.
