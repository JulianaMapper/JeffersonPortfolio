Northwestern: Building the Analytics Foundation for a $250M EHR Consolidation

Northwestern Medicine undertook one of the largest health system EHR consolidations in the region — a $250M, multi-year effort to unify disparate systems across 21 care locations into a single Epic platform. My role was to build the data and analytics layer that made the consolidated system legible to the entire organization. The technical challenge was real. The organizational one was harder.

The Problem

Northwestern Medicine had grown through acquisition and was operating multiple EHR systems across 21 care locations — each with its own data model, its own reporting structure, and its own definition of core clinical concepts. The organization had committed to a $250M, multi-year program to consolidate everything into a single Epic platform. That project would go live in March 2018.

The technical consolidation was underway. But there was a gap: the organization needed an analytics and reporting layer that could actually surface the value of that unified system to the people running it. Without it, you'd have one Epic instance and still no enterprise view — just a faster way to pull reports from one database instead of several. My role was to build the data infrastructure that made the consolidated system legible to the entire organization, from clinicians to department administrators.

The Decision

I led the strategy to build a unified enterprise data warehouse rather than attempting piecemeal integration of the four systems. Piecemeal integration produces a patchwork that nobody fully trusts — you end up with four sources of truth instead of one, just with bridges between them that break.

The harder decision was about access. The default approach to enterprise data warehouses is to make them the province of analysts — data goes in, reports come out, and frontline staff interact with a dashboard they didn't design and can't interrogate. I pushed for a different model: a single reporting portal with a user-facing experience designed for non-technical staff. The goal was to make every member of the organization a data user, not just a data consumer.

That meant significant organizational change management alongside the technical build — working with clinical and administrative leaders across 21 locations who had different needs, different vocabularies, and different levels of data fluency.

What I Built

An enterprise data warehouse strategy that consolidated all four Epic instances under a single data architecture — with a consistent data model, unified definitions for clinical and operational metrics, and a single source of truth for enterprise reporting.

A Tableau-integrated reporting portal with a custom user interface designed for non-technical staff. The goal was to make the data accessible enough that a department manager — not a data analyst — could answer their own operational questions without filing a report request. Search functionality, role-based access, and a design philosophy that prioritized legibility over completeness.

The organizational change management was as important as the technical architecture. Working with clinical leaders, department managers, and frontline staff across the system to understand what data they needed, why their current workflows existed, and how the new architecture could actually improve them rather than just disrupt them.

The Result

  • 21 care locations unified under one data architecture and one reporting platform, as part of Northwestern Medicine's $250M system-wide consolidation
  • Every member of the organization gained data access through a single, user-designed portal — not just analysts
  • Foundation laid for enterprise-wide analytics capability that subsequent data leadership could build on
  • Organizational change management completed across a geographically distributed, multi-speciality health system
"The technical problem at Northwestern wasn't integrating four databases. The technical problem was building something that 21 different care teams — with 21 different sets of workflows and expectations — would actually use. The database was the easier part."
— Jefferson McMillan-Wilhoit
