Northwestern: Unifying Four EHR Systems Across 21 Sites

One of the most complex health system data integrations in the region. The challenge wasn't just technical — it was organizational. How Jefferson built the data strategy and the reporting platform that unified it all.

The Problem

Northwestern's health network had grown through acquisition and affiliation — the natural result of a major academic medical center expanding its footprint across the Chicago region. The consequence was a fragmented data landscape: four different EHR systems, across 21 care locations, with no shared data model and no unified way for leadership, clinicians, or administrators to understand what was happening across the full system.

The problem wasn't unique. Almost every large health network that grew through acquisition faced the same thing. But the complexity at Northwestern — the scale, the number of systems, the organizational change management required — made it one of the most demanding integration challenges in the region.

The Approach

Jefferson's first insight was that the problem wasn't primarily technical. The technology to build an enterprise data warehouse existed. The harder challenge was organizational: getting 21 care locations, each with their own workflows, their own data definitions, and their own institutional identities, to agree on a shared data model.

He built the data warehouse strategy from the outside in — starting with the questions that leadership and clinicians needed to answer, not with the data that happened to be available. What does a clinical leader need to see to manage population health across 21 locations? What does an administrator need to understand operational performance? What does a quality officer need to track outcomes? Those questions defined the data model. The technical integration followed.

The Reporting Platform

The data warehouse alone wasn't sufficient — Jefferson also led development of an organization-wide reporting portal that made the unified data accessible to every level of the organization. The portal integrated Tableau with custom search functionality and a user experience designed for non-technical users.

The goal was self-service analytics: a department head should be able to answer their own operational questions without waiting for a data analyst to run a report. A clinical leader should be able to track their population health metrics in real time. That required both technical architecture and significant user experience design — making complex data accessible to people who hadn't been trained to use it.

What This Shows

The Northwestern engagement is the clearest example in Jefferson's career of the distinction between technical complexity and organizational complexity — and his ability to navigate both simultaneously. The data warehouse was a significant technical achievement. The organizational change management required to get 21 locations aligned around a shared data model was harder.

Jefferson has operated at the intersection of those two challenges — technical architecture and organizational alignment — throughout his career. It's the combination that makes health IT transformation actually work, as opposed to producing technically correct systems that nobody uses.
