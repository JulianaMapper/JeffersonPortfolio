The through line

One question has driven
every role: who is the
system failing?

Jefferson trained as an aeronautics engineer. He learned to ask not what failed, but who was in the cockpit when it did — and why the system let it happen.

That question followed him through pediatric informatics at Children's National, enterprise data architecture at Northwestern Medicine, CIO leadership through COVID at Lake County, and building a HIPAA-compliant AI platform from scratch.

Every project starts the same way: find the gap between what the system does and what the person in front of it actually needs. Then close it.

"The clinical data told me what happened to each child. The map told me why it kept happening. Those are two different questions — and only one of them lets you actually stop it."
01
Embry-Riddle · Aviation Safety
Trained in human factors and accident analysis. Learned that most system failures have a human at the center — and a design that failed them first.
02
Children's National · Pediatric Informatics
GIS mapping of burn injuries traced a pattern to a single landlord. Data didn't just describe the problem — it named it. Patent awarded.
03
Northwestern Medicine · Enterprise Analytics
Unified 4 EHR systems across 21 care sites. The harder problem wasn't the databases — it was 21 teams with 21 different ideas of what "good data" meant.
04
Lake County Health Dept · CIO & CDO
Built AllVax in 8 weeks on Salesforce. 40K registrations in 24 hours. 40% reduction in COVID death disparities. GovTech. ABC7. BT150.
05
F&T Labs · Founder & CEO
Built PH360 — a 7-layer, HIPAA-compliant, multi-tenant AI platform on AWS — because the market didn't have what public health agencies needed. $22K → $500K+ in year one.
