AllVax: Nation's Leading Vaccine Orchestration Platform

A county health department. An 8-week deadline. A pandemic. How Jefferson designed and built a vaccine scheduling system that logged 40,000 registrations in 24 hours — with equity as the design constraint, not the afterthought.

The Problem

In late 2020, Lake County Health Department faced a challenge shared by every public health agency in the country: how do you equitably distribute a limited supply of COVID-19 vaccines to a county of 700,000 people — including communities that historically don't trust government systems, can't navigate complex digital interfaces, or don't have reliable internet access?

The existing systems weren't built for this. Most scheduling platforms assumed digital literacy, English fluency, and internet access. They were designed for people who already had a relationship with the healthcare system — not for the communities that needed the vaccine most urgently.

As CIO and CDO, Jefferson's job was to build something better. Fast.

The Approach: Equity as the Design Constraint

Jefferson's first decision was a values decision: equity would be the design constraint, not a feature added at the end. That meant co-designing the platform with the communities it was meant to serve before a single line of code was written.

Jefferson and his team partnered with church groups, Latinx community leaders, and disability advocates — not to satisfy a checkbox, but because those communities knew things about their own barriers that no product manager at a tech company would ever discover in a user research session.

What they heard: people were scared of government data collection. They needed to register in their own language. They needed to be able to use the system on a touchscreen in freezing temperatures with gloves on. They needed a reason to trust that their information would be used to help them, not surveilled.

Every one of those requirements became a specification.

"We wanted to breed trust with our community. People see a large government entity trying to collect a whole bunch of information on them and they get very scared very quickly."
— Jefferson McMillan-Wilhoit, quoted in Slalom customer story

The Build

Jefferson selected and architected the AllVax platform on Salesforce Customer 360 for Government — leveraging Experience Cloud, Health Cloud, Marketing Cloud, MuleSoft, Tableau, and Shield in an integrated configuration purpose-built for public health equity.

The system was built in 8 weeks from concept to live. Key capabilities included:

  • Multilingual interface — available in multiple languages at launch
  • ADA-accessible design — screen reader compatible; touchscreen-optimized for cold-weather use
  • Automated state/federal reporting via MuleSoft — eliminated manual data entry entirely
  • Real-time equity monitoring via Tableau — allowed the team to identify and correct disparities in vaccination rates in near-real-time
  • Privacy-forward data model — designed to collect only what was necessary, communicated clearly to registrants

The Outcome

40,000 community members registered in the first 24 hours. 200,000+ in the first weeks — 25% of the county's entire population. Lake County was the last county in Illinois to record its first COVID-19 death, a testament to the speed and strategic coordination of the department's pandemic response.

Within one year, COVID-19 death disparities between Black, white, and Hispanic residents were reduced by 40%. Participation in health decision-making among historically underrepresented communities increased by 250%.

The story earned national coverage in State Tech Magazine, GovTech, and ABC News Chicago. Jefferson was quoted extensively on the equity design philosophy and the technology approach.

What This Shows

AllVax is the clearest illustration of what it looks like when an operator builds something. Jefferson wasn't a consultant parachuted in to implement a vendor's platform. He was the CIO who understood the community, defined the equity requirements, selected the architecture, managed the team, and was accountable for the outcome when the system went live at midnight.

That's a different kind of ownership than most technology leaders ever experience — and it's the foundation of everything he builds now.
